# Financial Aid Overview for Parents

> **Note:** Financial aid rules change frequently. Always verify current information on official websites like studentaid.gov. This overview provides general guidance as of 2026.

## Types of Financial Aid

### Grants & Scholarships (Free Money — Don't Have to Repay!)
- **Federal Pell Grant** — Need-based, up to ~$7,400/year (amount changes annually)
- **State grants** — Vary by state; check your state's higher education agency
- **Institutional grants** — Offered directly by colleges based on need and/or merit
- **Private scholarships** — From organizations, companies, community groups
- **Merit scholarships** — Based on academics, activities, talents (not financial need)

### Loans (Must Be Repaid)
- **Federal Direct Loans** — Lower interest rates, flexible repayment; always borrow federal first
- **Parent PLUS Loans** — Parents borrow for child's education; higher interest rates
- **Private Loans** — Last resort; higher rates, less flexible

### Work-Study
- **Federal Work-Study** — Part-time campus jobs for students with financial need
- Helps with expenses without taking on more debt

## Key Timelines
| When | What |
|------|------|
| Starting 9th grade | Begin researching scholarship opportunities |
| 10th grade | Attend financial aid workshops, use net price calculators |
| 11th grade | Research each college's financial aid policies, begin scholarship applications |
| October 1 of 12th grade | FAFSA opens — submit ASAP (some aid is first-come, first-served) |
| October-March of 12th grade | Submit CSS Profile if required by your colleges |
| March-April of 12th grade | Compare financial aid award letters carefully |
| May 1 | National Decision Day — commit to a college |

## FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid)
- **Required** for federal grants, loans, and work-study
- Opens **October 1** each year
- Uses tax information from two years prior
- Must be renewed every year of college
- Filing early can increase your aid — don't wait!

## What Affects Financial Aid?
- **Family income and assets** (biggest factor for need-based aid)
- **Number of family members in college** (can increase aid eligibility)
- **Student's academic record** (affects merit-based aid)
- **The college itself** (each school has different aid budgets and policies)

## Tips for Families
- **File FAFSA even if you think you earn too much** — you may be surprised
- **Use Net Price Calculators** — Every college has one on their website; gives estimated cost
- **Compare award letters carefully** — Look at the NET cost (total cost minus all free money)
- **Don't rule out expensive schools** — Many offer generous aid; the sticker price isn't the real price
- **Apply for EVERY scholarship** — Even small ones add up ($500 here, $1000 there)
- **Avoid scholarship scams** — Never pay to apply for a scholarship
- **Talk to your child about money** — Age-appropriately discuss what your family can contribute
